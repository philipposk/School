### [0:30-5:00] WHAT IS AN ARGUMENT?

**VISUAL:** Whiteboard animation showing premise â†’ conclusion structure

**INSTRUCTOR:** "In critical thinking, an argument is simply a set of statements where some statements - called premises - are offered as support for another statement - the conclusion."

**EXAMPLE VISUAL:**
- Premise 1: Exercise improves cardiovascular health
- Premise 2: Activities that improve cardiovascular health reduce mortality risk
- Conclusion: Therefore, exercise reduces mortality risk

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Notice how this isn't about conflict. It's about structure. It's about reasons supporting a claim."

### [5:00-10:00] PREMISE AND CONCLUSION INDICATORS

**VISUAL:** Interactive quiz-style graphics

**INSTRUCTOR:** "How do we spot arguments in everyday language? We look for indicator words."

**PREMIUM INDICATORS VISUAL:**
- Because
- Since
- Given that
- For the reason that

**CONCLUSION INDICATORS VISUAL:**
- Therefore
- Thus
- So
- Hence
- It follows that

**PRACTICE EXAMPLE:**
"Since the battery is old, and the engine won't start, the battery must be dead."

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Can you spot the indicators and structure here?"

---

### [10:00-15:00] VALIDITY VS SOUNDNESS

**VISUAL:** Side-by-side comparison with examples

**INSTRUCTOR:** "This is a crucial distinction. Validity is about structure. Soundness is about truth."

**VALID EXAMPLE:**
"All animals can fly. Cats are animals. Therefore, cats can fly."

**INSTRUCTOR:** "This is valid! If the premises were true, the conclusion would have to be true. But it's not sound because the premises are false."

**SOUND EXAMPLE:**
"All mammals are warm-blooded. Whales are mammals. Therefore, whales are warm-blooded."

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Both valid AND sound - proper structure AND true premises."

---

### [15:00-20:00] ARGUMENT VS EXPLANATION

**VISUAL:** Comparison table with real-world examples

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Arguments try to prove something. Explanations clarify something we already accept."

**ARGUMENT EXAMPLE:**
"You should wear a helmet because it reduces injury risk."

**EXPLANATION EXAMPLE:** 
"You fell because the road was slippery."

**INSTRUCTOR:** "One tries to persuade, the other helps understand."

---

### [20:00-25:00] HIDDEN ASSUMPTIONS

**VISUAL:** Peeling back layers animation

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Most arguments contain unstated assumptions. Finding these is superpower-level critical thinking."

**EXAMPLE:**
"You shouldn't trust that study because it's not peer-reviewed."

**INSTRUCTOR:** "Hidden assumption: Peer review is a reliable indicator of study quality. Is this always true?"
